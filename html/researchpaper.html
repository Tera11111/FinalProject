Music: The Face of the Past and the Hope of the Future

Written by Teralyn Whipple on November 8, 2019

Although they may not look it, the faded hopeful rockstar, the sulky teen jamming headphones in his ears and the world-renowned concert musician have something in common. In fact, they have something in common with just about the entire population of the world; they esteem music to be a fundamental aspect of their life. Nearly every person has experienced the throb of their heart in time with the beating of the drums or the tingles cascading through their bones as the last cadence of a piece spirals through the air of the concert hall. Even the phrase, “music is the universal language of mankind,” typically attributed to Henry Longsworth Longfellow, is plastered on the side of the subway, the grocery bag you carry and the living room wall. This love of music is so common that “even newborns exhibit a strong tendency to direct their attention more towards speech sounds with exaggerated pitch excursions than to those without this feature” (Masataka, 37). What would Star Wars be without the triumphant epithet as the villain enters the stage? Or titanic without the haunting melody of the love interest? What would Harry Potter be without the mystical swirls of strings that set the scene of magic and intrigue? Music forms the world we live in, it is a consistent, omnipresent force in our lives whether we are continuously aware of it or not. This, however, is not the basis of my argument, I only seek to understand the source of our infatuation with music. Why do we feel the way we do about music? Why does our heart pound with the beat? Why is music so prevalent throughout society across the world? And how can we utilize music to our benefit? I would like to advance the idea that the evolution of the human genome can disclose the secret to our everlasting captivation with music. I will focus on evidence supporting the idea that music has been a prominent approach to communicating emotion more than conversation for the bulk of human history.

To begin, I would like to evaluate the influence that music has on the human mind, starting by examining the early stages of our developing brain. According to research, we as a human race are able to process the individual components of music at a very young age, infants often mimic the style and contours of music while trying to communicate. This poses an interesting hypothesis that infants may recognize music as a form of communicating their primitive needs or emotions. In fact, a recent study of music proposed “that music evolved from the call signals of our hominid ancestors as… essentially, a mechanism for transforming emotional mental states efficiently and adaptively into social signals” (Clark, 1). Not only this, but we have a sensitivity towards the emotion portrayed by music through means such as tempo, rhythm, and pitch, “infants can produce body movements in response to the musical rhythm and they are sensitive to changes in tempo and rhythm” (Palmer, 1). It is clear in the article published by the Encyclopedia of Cognitive Science that a child naturally connects with and, at some level, understands music, indicating that music is a primal part of our existence rather than a learned trait through society or culture.

However, this sensitivity to music is not specific to impressionable children, “brain function [of any person] physically changes in response to music” (Turner, 1). According to the Gale Encyclopedia of Children’s Health, Music can influence physical properties such as breathing rate, heart rate, blood pressure, muscle tension but also motor skills, chemical reactions, and stress release. After many studies, scientists found that music can improve mental capacity while simultaneously assisting with relaxation, memory, and learning. “This effect may also be partially due to increased concentration that many people have while listening to music” (Turner, 1). Not only this but “the ability of music to influence human emotion is well supported by research” (Turner, 1). This evidence supports the idea that emotion is better presented through musical form and it may tie back to the previous claim that “music evolved from the call signals of our hominid ancestors… for transforming emotional mental states efficiently” (Clark, 1). Through research, we have been able to develop our understanding of how music can improve the physical, mental, and emotional health of a person. Nearly every section of the human brain is influenced, with the increase of memory, faster connections, pain reduction and stress relief. We have even found evidence that music can help cure diseases and illnesses (Trainor). Understanding the wide variety of responses resulting from music emphasizes the importance of learning what music is to us and how we can utilize it to improve society and build strong connections with diverse groups of people across the globe.

Through research, we have learned how music affects our bodies but there is still the question as to why we react this way, which can be answered by looking at our evolutionary development. There are many scientific studies explaining that the earliest form of language was more musical than linguistic and we already established that “at an early stage of development, human infants perceive speech sounds as music and are more likely to attend to the melodic and the rhythmic aspects of speech” (Masataka, 38). In fact, humans have been found to mimic and understand musical techniques “before they are able to produce well-articulated sounds” (Masataka, 36). Why is it that children seem to grasp the concept of music well before understanding more precise spoken language? To understand the evolutionary explanation behind this phenomenon, we must turn our attention to our ancestors; primates. “Long-distance calls produced by apes are commonly characterized by pure tonal notes… phrases… notes, accelerando in note rhythm…” (Masataka, 37) which all bear semblance to music in the human world and the type of musical quality human children mimic constantly in their developing stages. Darwin, the father of evolution, upon understanding this concept argued that “primeval man, or rather some early progenitor of man, probably first used his voice in producing true musical cadences, that is in singing”. According to more recent research, each species has developed its own musical quality for communicating that better suits their needs, giving evidence to the idea that music has evolved from prehistoric days. Apes give us insight into how our minds work and “while the production of multisyllabic calls by apes has conventionally been termed ‘singing’, more characteristics provided with the sounds are also recognized in singing by modern humans, regardless of the culture in which they live” (Masataka, 37). Additionally, “similar findings of [infantile connection with music] have been reported in some nonhuman primates” (Masataka, 37) indicating that we can assume a similarity in evolutionary development and cause. Studying primates gives us answers to why “human infants are innately predisposed to discover the particular patterned input of phonetic and syllabic units… extensively shared with some nonhuman primate[s]” (Masataka, 38).

Evolution is not exclusive; regardless of geographical location, culture or exposure to the outside world, every branch of humanity has similarities with their own music, there is no denying that music is a universal language. According to the research surrounding the spread of humans across the globe, humanity originated in the middle east area of the world. From there, our ancestors migrated and evolved, developing unique cultures, customs, and ethics. Despite the wide variety of beliefs across the world, humanity retains similarities that define who we are, not the least of which is our music. This widespread fascination could only be explained if our early ancestors developed this connection which distributed through the generations to our modern culture. Our early ancestors provide us the reason that music is forced through every speaker and blasted at every party, they are the reason sacred and important ceremonies across the globe utilize music as a main component. Understanding evolution explains our modern tendencies, Masataka said it best, “it is therefore possible that the loud calls of early hominids shared the above characteristics with apes… language competence in our ancestors is no longer puzzling.” We already understand a small portion of how music can benefit us in nearly every aspect of our lives, and now we have a good idea why. Music was developed primarily as a way to communicate emotion among species and as such, humanity has a deep, and even cellular connection with it even as language has matured into a more precise form of communicating. This millennia- deep connection through biology gives music the power to influence us in our behavior and thinking even today.

Despite all of this, there are those that argue that music is not an inherent component of our chemical makeup, but is instead a “cultural creation that can stimulate pleasure centers in the brain, a byproduct of the evolution of language” (Trainor, 1). As Steven Pinker famously stated, “music is auditory cheesecake, an exquisite confection crafted to tickle the sensitive spots [of the brain]”. Pinker and others would argue that music is solely a happy accident that happens to provide a temporary feeling of pleasure. Although it is true that this feeling of pleasure exists and is powerful, other scientists studying this phenomenon propose that music is much more than a coincidence. Millenia of evolution has prepared us to experience the joy in music that we do and intensive studies with our primate cousins give proof that music is not a concept unique to humanity. Apes use their own form of music created through variety in pitch and rhythm to communicate, not unlike humans do with our more evolved form of music theory. Our ancestors used music in a similar way to primates and we have maintained this connection with our roots. Additionally, the widespread influence of music gives us evidence that there is more at work than culture and tradition.

Along the same line, music itself has evolved from its crude beginning form, seen in our history as it gradually gains complexity and layered emotion that seems to be a metaphor of the human condition itself. Since the beginning, composers have used music as a way of “preserving the valued heritage of the intellectual literary tradition” (M., 1). As we can see, music can be largely impacted by cultural influences, and in fact, it seems to mimic historical accounts. As an illustration, music follows composing eras that can be used to define historical time periods such as the baroque period, the romantic period and even the rock craze. Ultimately, whether we wish it or not, music is directly tied to human happenings. Indeed, one could read the history of the world in a roadmap of the music footprint we make, seen in musical reform movements of the 50s and 60s, war songs in the 40s and patriotic ballads of the civil and revolutionary wars. While someone may argue, “we all eat; we enjoy good food and have good cultures, just as we have musical cultures… isn’t surprising as evolving an enjoyment of eating” (Mithens, 3), I argue that this enjoyment of music comes from a deeper connection than mere passing enjoyment. There is a reason our bodies react physically to the vibrations of the air around us. While music is tied directly to our society, music was not a result of cultural events, music was developed from the ancient periods and evolved into something fundamentally connected to us that, should we ignore it, we would become lost, hence our historic ties to music.

Now that we have analyzed the history and development of music, we can delve into how we can utilize music to our advantage. Music is unlike many other things that humans typically enjoy, for example, food or sex. Both of these things are self- explanatory for the survival of the human race but music has to be examined with a more critical eye. We have previously discovered what music can offer us; things like mental stability, physical health, and an increase in brain activity. I propose that we examine more thoroughly at what this knowledge can do for humanity rather than the specific, individual benefits. The first thing I would like to consider is the communicating aspect of music. As we already discovered, music evolved from the primitive attempts of our ancestors to communicate to others and this purpose has not faded over the millennia. Using the “universal language” that we have at our fingertips, we can reach out to people across the world and share our emotions and experiences. Through music, we can broaden the minds of people around the world and develop a brotherhood of sensitive people who fight for and learn from each other. I firmly believe that music has this power if we give it the chance. We see this very same power in the month of December when strangers gather to sing hymns and carols and hockey games when bleachers rise for the national anthem. We should never underestimate the power that music has to connect people.

The next subject I would like to address is using music more often to heal people of various ailments. Music is already used in music therapy for these very reasons, but I believe that music could be utilized to the greater advantage if medical professionals understood the deep connection humanity has with it. Studying evolution beyond the physical properties of our bodies will help us understand and treat our minds better. The impact music has and could have for the good of humanity is revolutionary and important for us to continue to cultivate as we develop our understanding of evolution.

The ability that music has to inspire empathy in nearly anyone is as prodigious as it is unique. Music is undeniably connected to humans in some way; we can see this in the individual development of styles and characteristics across the world. We can feel this when we listen to a powerful piece or song and we get chills or when we feel our blood pumping to the beat of a drum in our ears. We can even see it in marketing techniques and devices available for purchase online, many of which are targeted towards enjoying music. Contrary to common belief, our modern world did not invent this concept, from the beginning of history humans have been finding ways to use the objects around them to create and invent. There is a deep connection in each of us that draws us to the mystic of music, and I contend that this is due to evolution. Not only does music portray and communicate emotion across cultures and backgrounds, but it can help physically heal a person. The evolutionary power that music has to inspire our earnest and deepest emotions is not only unique but important to our society. Music can help us realize ourselves and has a specific function in each person's life. Mithens said it best in his article, “a key part of being human is being musical” (Mithens, 4). The evolution of the human genome discloses why we as a species retain a captivation with music, explained primarily through our primitive need for communicating that evolved into a powerful tool of reforming minds and deepening interpersonal and intrapersonal connections.
